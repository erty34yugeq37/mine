CODE University Berlin – Personal Statement

I've always figured things out by doing them. In school that's genuinely difficult to act on — the whole system is set up around listening and reproducing, and if you step sideways from that you mostly just fall behind. So I worked within it, got through it, but I never stopped looking for somewhere that actually matched how I think.

I found CODE through a forum post, not through any official channel. Someone was describing their first semester and I remember thinking it didn't sound like a university at all — they were just building something from the first week. No modules to tick off first. That got my attention.

The Quest model makes sense to me in a way that most "project-based" course descriptions don't. A lot of places say that and then you look at the structure and there's still a lecture series underneath it. What I understand about CODE is that the project isn't a wrapper around a traditional curriculum — it is the curriculum. You pick a real problem, you have to make something, and the learning is what happens when you're stuck. That's how I've always actually learned things, so finding a degree built around it was — I don't know, a bit of a relief I guess.

The thing about the three programmes — Software Engineering, Interaction Design, Product Management — not being kept apart is also something I care about more than I expected when I first looked into it. I've spent enough time on things that don't work to know that the problem is usually at the boundary between disciplines, not inside any one of them. A product doesn't care which department owns which part. It either holds together or it doesn't. I'm not interested in only understanding my corner of it.

Berlin is worth mentioning too, though I'm aware it can sound like I'm just gesturing at the city. What I actually mean is more about where CODE is embedded. Neukölln is not a campus at a remove from everything — it's in a place where people are actively building things, and from what I understand those people are involved in how students learn, not just invited occasionally to give a talk. That's a different setup and it matters to how much of the real thing you're actually exposed to.

I don't know exactly what I'll end up building. But I know how I work, and I think CODE is set up for people who work the way I do. That's more than I've been able to say about anywhere else.
